About the project

Artificial intelligence (AI) is the technology that enables machines to perform tasks that require human intelligence, such as learning and decision-making. It has broad applications, including health, industry, transportation, finance, and entertainment, making it a valuable tool for understanding complex issues.

With the support of artificial intelligence, the AquaSeal project was born, a web platform developed to help understand the parameters that define quality water. This is an academic initiative led by students from the Tiradentes University Center, integrating different areas of knowledge in an interdisciplinary project.

The team was motivated to explore this area after realizing the lack of transparency in the results of water quality tests, often provided by private companies that work in partnership with the government. AquaSeal combines disciplines such as back-end, Data Science and Artificial Intelligence, as well as Software Testing, so that students apply this knowledge to develop a tool that uses AI to process data and provide reliable information about water quality, promoting an accessible, educational, and transparent analysis for society.

How to perform the tests?

To take the test, simply enter the requested data in the respective fields. If you do not know any of them, leave it blank. The system will use AI to process and compensate for this lack. However, all the requested data is essential for a more accurate result, so if you leave a field blank, the answer may be less accurate.

Data that will be passed to AI:

Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD)
Biochemical Oxygen Demand measures the amount of oxygen required to break down organic matter in the water. High values indicate organic contamination, which can lead to the proliferation of harmful microorganisms.
Chloride
The concentration of chlorides, usually derived from natural minerals or industrial and domestic pollution, is an important parameter for potability. Elevated levels can cause a salty taste in water and potential long-term health impacts.
Conductivity
Conductivity measures the water's ability to conduct electricity, which is related to the presence of dissolved ions. High values indicate large concentrations of dissolved salts or contaminants that may be harmful to health.
Dissolved Oxygen (DO)
Dissolved oxygen in water is essential for aquatic life and is a quality indicator. Low oxygen levels may suggest pollution or stagnant water, as well as conditions unsuitable for human consumption.
Orthophosphate (as P)
The concentration of phosphates, generally derived from fertilizers or detergents, is a relevant parameter for potability. High levels can lead to eutrophication, resulting in water treatment problems and changes in taste.
pH
pH measures the acidity or alkalinity of water. To be considered potable, water must have a pH between 6.5 and 8.5, ensuring it is not corrosive to the distribution system and is safe for consumption.
Temperature
Water temperature influences the solubility of gases and the efficiency of treatment processes. Elevated temperatures can increase microbial activity, compromising water quality for consumption.
Total Alkalinity (as CaCO₃)
Total alkalinity measures the water's ability to neutralize acids, consisting of bicarbonates, carbonates, and occasionally hydroxides. It is an important parameter because it indicates the water's pH stability, which is essential to prevent pipe corrosion and ensure water safety for consumption. Adequate levels are also crucial for chemical water treatment.
Total Ammonia (as N)
The concentration of ammonia in water, often resulting from the decomposition of organic matter or pollution, is a quality indicator. High levels can be toxic and indicate contamination from sewage or industrial waste. Additionally, excess ammonia can hinder disinfection processes like chlorination.
Total Hardness (as CaCO₃)
Total hardness measures the concentration of calcium and magnesium dissolved in water. Very hard water can cause scaling in pipes, while very soft water can be corrosive, impacting water quality and distribution.
True Color
The true color of water, measured after the removal of suspended particles, is a visual quality indicator. Color changes may indicate the presence of organic matter, metals, or pollution, affecting consumer acceptance and treatment efficiency.
